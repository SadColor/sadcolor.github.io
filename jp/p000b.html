Pitch accent patterns

{{ imgR }} {{ frog_n }}
In Japanese, words can have a downstep in pitch, or not.
Whether and where the downstep occurs gives rise to the four pitch accent patterns: 平板へいばん (flat), 頭高あたまだか (head-high), 中高なかだか (middle-high), and 尾高おだか (tail-high).
Particles don't have an inherent pitch, but instead keep the same pitch as the preceding word.
Let's start with atamadaka, because it's different than all the others.
Atamadaka words start with a high pitch on the first mora, then fall in pitch on the second and stay at the low pitch. In other words, the accent falls on the first mora.
Here are some examples of atamadaka words. Try listening to native speakers say these words or put them into Suzuki-kun and mimicking the pitch. {{ triR }} {{ reibuntop }}
秋（あ↘き） 天気（て↘んき） 文学（ぶ↘んがく） 箸（は↘し）
autumn weather literature chopsticks
季節（き↘せつ） 挨拶（あ↘いさつ） 竜（りゅ↘う） 生徒（せ↘いと）
season greeting dragon student
{{ cornerR }}

Even one-mora words can be atamadaka.
Pitch accent isn't strictly contained within the word, so a one-mora word like 木き will be said on the high pitch, but you can't hear the low pitch until the following word or particle. So for example, if we attach the が particle, the pitch becomes 木き↘が.
Actually, I think these can also be called odaka, a pattern we're about to cover.

{{ triR }}
{{ imgL }} {{ frog_t }}

For the other three patterns, the first mora is a low pitch, followed by a high pitch for the rest of the mora until the downstep.
Heiban words make up a little over 50% of Japanese words. In this pattern, there is never a downstep. In this pattern, only the first mora is low, and the rest are high. Note that the pitch rise on heiban words isn't as great as on the other patterns.
Nakadaka words have the downstep fall before the end of the word. Therefore, the pattern is a low mora, followed by at least one high mora until the downstep.
Odaka words have the downstep fall on the last mora of the word. This means that they sound identical to the heiban pattern, until you hear a particle or other word come afterwards, when you will hear the pitch drop.
Here are examples of these three patterns. Try to identify which are which based on where the downstep occurs.

昨夜（さ↗く↘や） 弟（お↗とうと↘） 三日月（み↗かづき） 地獄（じ↗ごく↘）
last night younger brother crescent moon hell
蜂蜜（は↗ちみつ） 橋（は↗し↘） 台風（た↗いふ↘う） 端（は↗し）
honey bridge typhoon edge
卵（た↗ま↘ご） 説明（せ↗つめい） 心（こ↗ころ↘） 新幹線（し↗んか↘んせん）

Well, knowing this is all good and well, but if you want to put this knowledge to use, you're gonna have to pay attention when you hear Japanese speech and see how their pitch changes throughout the sentence, and to where pitches change inside individual words.
The key factor is the downstep. By listening to a word and the particle that follows, you can class words in to heiban (no downstep), atamadaka (downstep after the first mora), nakadaka (downstep in the middle of the word), or odaka (downstep after the end of the word).
To help you start listening for accent, try listening for pitch differences between these similar words.

Pitch accent of い adjectives and verbs

{{ imgL }} {{ frog_t }}

Pitch accent for い adjectives and verbs are actually simpler, in some sense.
I think nearly every verb and い adjective, you can classify them as either heiban or not heiban. Unfortunately, the where the downstep occurs can change depending on the inflection,
For heiban verbs, the a downstep occurs on the ま↘す in the polite form, on the な↘かった in the negative past form, on the え↘ば in the ba form, and the お↘う in the volitional form.
For non-heiban verbs, the downstep always occurs on the second-to-last mora (for example, つく↘る). The downstep shifts backwards one mora for the past, negative, negative past, ba-, and te-forms.
For heiban adjectives, a downstep is added before the い (or whatever replaces the い in reflections that replace it) in every inflection besides the く (adverbial) form. This also includes when you use です, for example あ↗か↘いです when the normal pitch accent is あ↗かい.
For non-heiban adjectives, the downstep will always fall on the mora before the い. You can actually keep the pitch accent the same across all inflections. It's also valid to shift the downstep back one mora just like non-heiban verbs in every inflection besides the polite form.
You haven't learned any of these inflections for verbs and adjectives, but in the future you can reference this section for the rules.
