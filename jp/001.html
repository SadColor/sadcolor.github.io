Hiya! Welcome to SadColor's Japanese grammar lessons!
Today, we'll make some very basic, but useful sentences!

what is a sentence, anyhow?

In English, I would say our conception of a complete sentence relates to our idea of a complete thought. If a sentence is missing information, it is incomplete unless there is some immediate context for it (like being asked a question).

泣な く。
Cries.
かっこいい。
Cool.
帰かえ る。
Will go home.

These sentences are completely fine in Japanese, despite being mere fragments in English.
Japanese is a much more context-sensitive language, so you're expected to fill in more information yourself compared to English. Of course, context can still change how you interpret things.
We'll talk about some of that information-dropping just a little bit later, though.

topics and comments

Japanese is considered a topic–comment language.
A topic is what's being talked about and a comment is what's being said about a topic.
How that differs from subject and predicate is that topic–comment is more pragmatic (a topic can persist over several comments), while subject and predicate are matters of sentence structure (they're identifiable in each individual sentence). Here's an example to help illustrate the difference.

Have you seen Tam recently?
Here, the topic is Tam, but "you" is the subject.
I haven't! I wonder what they're up to.
We're still discussing Tam here, but the subject is "I" in both of these sentences.
Oh, they're on vacation right now.
Here, the subject and topic line up!

Hopefully that helps you understand the concept better. We'll have to butt heads against it more in the future, but let's make a darn sentence already!

basic sentences

So, to construct the most simple of sentences, let's make friends with the はwa particle! The はwa particle lets us specificy a topic.
Particles in Japanese follow the word they modify, so our basic sentence structure will look like 「topicはcomment」.

猫ねこ は寝ね る。
Cries.
ここは地獄じごく 。
This is hell.
ポケモンは面白おもしろ い。
Pokémon is interesting.

When translating extra-literally, Xはwa is usually translated to "as for X" or "regarding X" or the like. It's almost never a natural-sounding translation, but it can be good for reinforcing that idea of topic that isn't quite so prominent in English.

why is the はwa particle written はha and not わwa ?

Historically, when written in the middle of a word, the H-row kana はひふへほha hi fu he ho were actually pronounced わいうえおwa i u e o .
I think since particles functionally considered "attached" to the words they modify, "wa" was written as は and not わ.
Japanese script reformation began in the Meiji period, and after World War II, one such reform targeted the issue of kana spelling, which changed spelling to be closer to modern pronunciation. This is when the ゐヰwi and ゑヱwe kana became obsolete.
For example, 川river in kana would now be spelled かわkawa instead of かはkaha (as mentioned, it had already been pronounced "kawa" before the spelling changed).
However, a lot of words like 川 were usually written with kanji, and wouldn't be impacted by such a spelling reform. Particles, on the other hand, are always written with kana, so the decision was made to exclude は (along with へe and をo ) from being changed to a different spelling.

adding wrinkles: the subject marker

So, now that we have our topic, we can also talk about a specific subject using the がga particle.
The most common usage of the two particles is a sentence structure that goes something like 「topicはtrait of topicがcomment」. That probably doesn't make too much sense, so here are some more examples.

私わたし は姉あね がいる。
I have an older sister / I have older sisters.
(literally "Regarding me, older sister exists.")
この町まち は人口じんこう が少すく ない。
This town has a low population.
(literally "Regarding this town, population is few.")
果物くだもの はマンゴーが美味おい しい。
Mangoes are a delicious fruit.
(literally "Regarding fruit, mangoes are delicious.")
ヴェリティ―は眉毛まゆげ が長なが い。
Verity has long eyebrows.
(literally "Regarding Verity, eyebrows are long.")

to be or not to be

Unlike English, Japanese has an animacy distinction for its equivalent of our verb "to be".
For inanimate objects, use あるaru . For animate beings, use いるiru .

I think that also gives me the the chance to introduce the にni particle.
It has other uses, but the relevant one here is that it indicates the destination or target of some verbs.
You can use にni with ある and いる to state that something exists in a certain location.

99% of the time, you should use the がga particle with あるaru and いるiru .

I don't want to take too much time to explain it here, but at least while you're starting out, you almost always want to say 「●●がある」 and not 「●●はある」.
Intransitive verbs, especially ones that refer back to the subject, almost always prefer が.
When you say something like 「●●はある」, it has a specific nuance.
We'll cover that nuance more in a later article.

location particle… two!!

I should probably mention this too while I'm at it, but the にni particle does not indicate the location for most verbs, when it comes to a big-picture location. When you want to say a sentence like "I watch TV at home", you need the でde particle.

家いえ でテレビを見み る。
I watch TV at home.
(cannot say 家いえ に)

Note that for some verbs, they can still use the にni particle to indicate something you could also call a location, so it helps to remember that destination or target wording. In these cases, the にni particle will indicate a specific spot that is being directly affected by the action. I'm not really sure how to describe that precisely, so I think you'll understand if I just show ya some examples.

ここで書か いてください。
Please write here.
(you are being asked to write in a specific room, etc.)
ここに書か いてください。
Please write here.
(you are being asked to fill in a blank, etc.)
に。

youon

拗音ようおん Youon are the sounds written with the other small kana: ゃゅょァィゥェォ.
While youon take two kana to represent one syllable, they are still one syllable. A word like 東京とうきょう Tokyo is pronounced "to-kyo", not "to-ki-yo" like English speakers would pronounce it. りゃrya, りゅryu, and りょryo are all particularly difficult for English speakers to pronounce, so be sure to practice those ones in particular. Here are some common words using those sounds:

  • 略りゃくす ryakusu
  • 旅行りょこう ryokou
  • 無料むりょう muryou
  • 留学りゅうがく ryuugaku
  • 昇竜拳しょうりゅうけん shouryuuken

speech timing

In English, all words have what's called lexical stress; that is, words have certain syllables stressed in them in a specific way, and changing the stress can change the meaning of the word. For example: present (a noun or adjective) vs. present (a verb).
Japanese, on the other hand, does not have lexical stress, and you can think of speech as having a certain rhythm. With the exception of youon, all kana take up one "beat" in this rhythm, even if it doesn't constitute a separate syllable. Linguistically, these beats can be called "morae" or the Japanese-specific term * 音おん "on"*, but I'll be using the term "mora" since it doesn't overlap with any common English word.
Here are a few words to illustrate the difference between morae and syllables.

  • 墓haka はか, 2 morae / 2 syllables
  • 破壊hakai はかい, 3 morae / 2 syllables
  • 発火hakka はっか, 3 morae / 2 syllables
  • 発会hakkai はっかい, 4 morae / 2 syllables

pitch accent

Japanese does feature something a little similar to English stress, which is pitch accent.
Pitch accent isn't strictly necessary to be understood, so I tend to view it as a sidequest if you want to get ever-closer to sounding closer to a native speaker.
