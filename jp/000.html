Let's learn how to actually pronounce Japanese words! Or, more accurately, let's learn about some quirks of Japanese pronunciation, I guess.

vowel sounds

Japanese has a more limited sound inventory than English, but that doesn't make it easy for us to pronounce! Let's start with the easier part: vowels.
In Japanese, there are only five vowel sounds: あ, い, う, え, and お. Here are some approximations that I think are more or less right:

  • あa is pronounced ah like the sound in bra. (IPA: [a])
  • いi is pronounced ee like the sound in tee. (IPA: [e])
  • うu is pronounced oo like the sound in who. (IPA: [ɯ])
  • えe is pronounced eh like the sound in set. (IPA: [o])
  • おo is pronounced oh like the sound in go. (IPA: [i])

An important note about Japanese vowels is that they're all unrounded.
In English, we round our lips when saying O and U, but that isn't the case in Japanese. It does affect the sound, so keep it in mind!
We also don't really have a monophthong O sound. Instead, that "long O" sound is actually a diphthong that glides from an O to a U. So getting that sound down pat will take just a pinch more effort than the others.

Japanese also has long vowels. They're a little like stressed syllables in English, but not that similar.
These do present a couple of the rare exceptions to Japanese spelling:

  • おうou is pronounced the same as おおoo.
  • えいei is pronounced the same as ええee.

Note that it's not strictly incorrect to pronounce words this way, but it's usually only done when being extra-precise.
In some romanization systems, long vowels are written with a circumflex (âîûêô) or macron (āīūēō). While these help people unfamiliar with Japanese not read "oo" or "ee" with the incorrect vowels, I'll be avoiding this particular convention in these articles.

vowel elision

When speaking, the うi and うu sounds are often not clearly voiced, especially near voiceless consonants (k, s, sh, t, ts). Here are some examples! Click each romanization to hear an audio sample, taken from Forvo.

  • 月つき "tsuki" → "ts-ki"
  • 地下鉄ちかてつ "ch-katetsu" → "ch-katets-"
    Note how on both speakers here don't really voice the i here.

Even one of the most well-known Japanese words, ですdesu, is actually almost always pronounced like "dess", especially when followed by other sentence-ending particles.
On another note, you generally don't devoice two vowels in a row, even it seems possible.

  • つつく "tsutsuku" → "ts-tsuku" and not "ts-ts-ku"
  • 歴史的れきしてき "rekishiteki" → "rek-shiteki"
    (Gonna be honest, I think this one is kinda borderline.)

I hope it's not complicating things too much to mention this aspect of pronunciation explicitly, but I think it comes pretty naturally once you can speak at a more regular pace.

the F sound

The sound romanized as F (only used with ふ・フfu) is actually pretty different to English.
In English, we put our bottom lip to our teeth and blow air through.
In Japanese, the F sound is made just by blowing air through your lips, without touching your teeth.
The IPA for this sound is [ɸ].

the "R" sound

A pretty well-known fact about Japanese is that there is no distinction between R and L. The sound romanized as "R" is, for English speakers, actually a lot closer to an "L" sound. Its IPA is [ɾ].
To make this sound, you touch your tongue to the roof of your mouth, right behind your teeth.
If you can roll your R's, you can also just do one "roll", which is a pretty close approximation (that's called a "flap"!).

the W sound

There's not much to say about this one.
The main point is that W is pronounced rather weakly compared to English, with much less lip-rounding.
When you say a word like 川かわ "kawa", your lips hardly change shape from the A sound to the W sound.
Consequently, you might hear words like 回まわる "mawaru" being pronounced almost like "ma-aru".

the ん sound

This one is trickier than you might expect, because it doesn't really have a fixed pronunciation! Most of the time, you can get away just pronouncing it as an N, but it's actually much fuzzier than that.
It's basically pronounced only using your nose, and not your tongue.
Notice the difference in pronunciation between here 千円せんえん "sen'en" and 千年せんねん "sennen".
The N sound is much more prominent in 千年せんねん "sennen", while in 千円せんえん "sen'en" it's much muddier. In fact, some people even pronounce 千円せんえん "sen'en" in a way that sounds like a "y" sound has snuck in!

the small "tsu"

The small tsu (っ or ッ) represents a geminate consonant. The Japanese term for it is 促音そくおん "sokuon". In plain English, that means you say the following consonant for longer.
Here are some examples to help illustrate that.

  • 坂さか saka — 作家さっか sakka
  • 首長しゅちょう shuchou — 出張しゅっちょう shucchou

The other use for the sokuon is at the end of an utterance, which indicates that it is cut off very sharply, also known as a glottal stop.

youon

拗音ようおん Youon are the sounds written with the other small kana: ゃゅょァィゥェォ.
While youon take two kana to represent one syllable, they are still one syllable. A word like 東京とうきょう Tokyo is pronounced "to-kyo", not "to-ki-yo" like English speakers would pronounce it. りゃrya, りゅryu, and りょryo are all particularly difficult for English speakers to pronounce, so be sure to practice those ones in particular. Here are some common words using those sounds:

  • 略りゃくす ryakusu (to abbreviate)
  • 旅行りょこう ryokou (travel)
  • 無料むりょう muryou (free of charge)
  • 留学りゅうがく ryuugaku (study abroad)
  • 昇竜拳しょうりゅうけん shouryuuken (Shoryuken)

speech timing

In English, all words have what's called lexical stress; that is, words have certain syllables stressed in them in a specific way, and changing the stress can change the meaning of the word. For example: present (a noun or adjective) vs. present (a verb).
Japanese, on the other hand, does not have lexical stress, and you can think of speech as having a certain rhythm. With the exception of youon, all kana take up one "beat" in this rhythm, even if it doesn't constitute a separate syllable. Linguistically, these beats can be called "morae" or the Japanese-specific term * 音おん "on"*, but I'll be using the term "mora" since it doesn't overlap with any common English word.
Here are a few words to illustrate the difference between morae and syllables.

  • 墓haka はか (grave), 2 morae / 2 syllables
  • 破壊hakai はかい (destruction), 3 morae / 2 syllables
  • 発火hakka はっか (ignition), 3 morae / 2 syllables
  • 発会hakkai はっかい (opening a meeting), 4 morae / 2 syllables

pitch accent

Japanese does feature something a little similar to English stress, which is pitch accent.
Pitch accent isn't strictly necessary to be understood, so I tend to view it as a sidequest if you want to get ever-closer to sounding closer to a native speaker.
