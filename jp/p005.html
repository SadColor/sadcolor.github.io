The three types of verbs

Japanese has three types of verbs: godan, ichidan, and irregular verbs.
Godan verbs make up most verbs of Japanese. Godan means "five row", and refers to the fact that through each of the conjugations, the final kana of the stem will change between all five vowel sounds. They're also known as consonant-stem verbs, because if you wrote out the conjugations in romaji, the part that remains constant would end in a consonant.
Ichidan verbs are the second most common. Ichidan means "one row", so in this case the final kana of the stem always remains the same, and any conjugations simply attach to the stem rather than modifying it. They're also called vowel-stem verbs following similar logic to consonant-stem verbs.
A notable fact about ichidan verbs is that they all end in an い-row or え-row kana followed by a る in the dictionary form. If it doesn't match this pattern, it's definitely a godan verb. If it does match this pattern it doesn't 100% mean it's an ichidan, though. You'll just have to learn what verbs are ichidan, and there are also homophones between dictionary-form ichidan and godan verbs (the conjugations will be different, though).

Now, for irregular verbs, there are kind of two types of irregular verbs?
Anyways, the verbs that are usually called just "irregular" are the highly irregular verbs する and 来くる. They're called "highly" irregular because none of their conjugations match what we would normally expect of a godan verb.
Besides those, there are a couple verbs that are only slightly irregular (ある, いく, くれる, 問とう), because only one of their conjugations are different than expected.
Lastly, this doesn't really fit anywhere else, but 言いう is often pronounced "ゆう" when spoken.

The non-past tense

Okay, so the dictionary form of any verb is often referred to as the non-past tense.
It's also often said to represent the imperfect aspect, since it represents an action that has not been completed. For most verbs, you don't use the non-past tense to represent a current, ongoing action (notable exceptions being ある and いる), making it almost effectively a future tense in most uses. We'll cover how to represent a presently ongoing action in just a bit.

Ren'youkei: The -masu stem

The 連用形れんようけい (conjunctive) form of a verb is also called the -masu stem of the verb, since it's the form of the verb that you attach ます to when making polite verbs. However, there are a few different things that "stem" can refer to in Japanese, so I'll just call it the ren'youkei for clarity's sake.
Before I get into how it's used, let's take a look at how it's formed.
For godan verbs, change the last kana of the dictionary form from the う-row kana to the い-row kana.
For ichidan verbs, simply remove the last る of the dictionary form kana.
する and 来くる, as always, have irregular inflections, changing to し and 来き.

The primary usage of the ren'youkei is that you can attach other verbs and verb endings to it (hence the english term "conjunctive").
Grammatically speaking, ます (what all polite form verbs end in) is just an auxiliary verb that joins to the ren'youkei to create the meaning of politeness. There are many such verbs that can attach to the ren'youkei, like 込こむ, 出だす, 切きる, that change the meaning of the root.
There are a lot of words that are formed by attaching a noun to the ren'youkei of a verb, too. Some really common ones are 食たべ物もの, 入いり口ぐち, and 出で口ぐち. 方かた is a suffix that can go on the end of a ren'youkei verb that gives it the meaning "way/method of (verb)ing".
For some verbs, the ren'youkei can also double as the noun form, such as in 代かわり (substitute), 負まけ (loss), or 踊おどり (dance).
And finally, the ren'youkei is also used in literary style to link two clauses of a sentence together. In speech or less formal writing, the て form is used for this instead.

The ない form: negation

Here's an uncomplicated form. The negative form of the verb, simply put, negates the verb! Like the English word "not" does.
For godan verbs, change the final kana to an あ-row kana, then add ない.
Note that for verbs that end with う, the う will become a わ instead of an あ (we'll see an example below).
For ichidan verbs, attach ない to the ren'youkei.
For polite verbs, attach ません to the ren'youkei.
For irregular verbs, する and 来くる change to しない and 来こない.
ある becomes just ない, instead of あらない.

EXAMPLE
木村きむらや雪子ゆきこは英語英語が話はなせる。 音楽おんがくはロックとかメタルとか（が）好すき。
Kimura and Yukiko (and others) can speak English. I like stuff like metal and rock music.
あの店みせはパンやケーキやクッキーなどを売うる。 蛍光けいこうペンとか付箋紙ふせんしなどは意外いがいと便利べんり 。
That shop sells bread, cakes, cookies, and so on. Highlighters and sticky notes are surprisingly useful.

There are a couple other ways to negate verbs that are common enough that you should know about them, even though you probably won't use them yourself 99% of the time.
ず and ぬ are alternate, archaic negations of verbs. They go in the same place that ない does on a normal negative verb.
The ぬ form works basically the same as a normal negative verb, but since it's older, it gives off a dramatic or old-timey feel, I guess. In casual speech, you often hear negative verbs ending in ん (for example, 分わからない → 分わからん), which is a contracted form of ぬ, rather than ない.
The ず form is mostly used adverbially (with or without the に particle), meaning "without doing".
Both of these forms persist in media like songs and shows, or in set phrases and idioms. One such set phrase is ざるを得えない ("cannot help doing" or "have no choice but to do"), which uses an inflection of ず.
The irregular する, which normally becomes しない, instead becomes せぬ and せず.
来くる becomes 来こぬ and 来こず, keeping the same negative reading from 来こない.
The verb ある, which normally irregularly becomes ない, actually inflects according to normal godan rules and becomes あらぬ and あらず.

EXAMPLE
ぷよぷよとかのパズルゲームが分わからない。 数十年すうじゅうねん前まえ、携帯けいたいなど存在そんざいしなかった。
I don't understand puzzle games like Puyo Puyo. Decades ago, there weren't things like cell phones.
迷信めいしんとか信しんじない。 あいつは人ひとの気持きもちなど気きにかけない。
I don't believe in superstitions or anything like that.
*using など will sound a little more dismissive of superstitions, I think		 They don't even care about people's feelings.

This isn't super useful to know, but if you're curious...
In older Japanese, the ず negation is the 終止形しゅうしけい, or terminal form. This means you use it at the end of a statement.
ぬ was the 連体形れんたいけい, or attributive form. That means you use it to modify a noun.
To connect these two forms to modern grammar, consider な adjectives. When they are used in attributive form, you have to use a な, like 些細ささいな誤解ごかい. But when you use them in terminal form (end of statement), you instead add だ, like 誤解ごかいは些細ささいだ.
On the other hand, い adjectives have the same attributive form and terminal form (ending in い), which means they don't change whether you put them in front of a noun or at the end of the sentence.
I mentioned earlier that the ん negative is actually a contraction of ぬ. As it turns out, the negative polite ません is a contraction of ませぬ! Ain't that funny.

Using the て form of nouns when somebody is multiple things

You might remember from the previous lesson on adjectives how the て form of な adjectives can be used to connect multiple adjectives together. No surprises, the exact same inflection is used for nouns too.
