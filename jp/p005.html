The three types of verbs

{{ imgR }} {{ frog_k }}

Japanese has three types of verbs: godan, ichidan, and irregular verbs.
Godan verbs make up most verbs of Japanese. Godan means "five row", and refers to the fact that through each of the conjugations, the final kana of the stem will change between all five vowel sounds. They're also known as consonant-stem verbs, because if you wrote out the conjugations in romaji, the part that remains constant would end in a consonant.
Ichidan verbs are the second most common. Ichidan means "one row", so in this case the final kana of the stem always remains the same, and any conjugations simply attach to the stem rather than modifying it. They're also called vowel-stem verbs following similar logic to consonant-stem verbs.
A notable fact about ichidan verbs is that they all end in an い-row or え-row kana followed by a る in the dictionary form. If it doesn't match this pattern, it's definitely a godan verb. If it does match this pattern it doesn't 100% mean it's an ichidan, though. You'll just have to learn what verbs are ichidan, and there are also homophones between dictionary-form ichidan and godan verbs (the conjugations will be different, though).
Here's a random list of some ichidan and godan verbs, complete with some homophones to look out for.

Now, for irregular verbs, there are kind of two types of irregular verbs?
Anyways, the verbs that are usually called just "irregular" are the highly irregular verbs する and 来くる. They're called "highly" irregular because none of their conjugations match what we would normally expect of a godan verb.
Besides those, there are a couple verbs that are only slightly irregular (ある, いく, くれる, 問とう), because only one of their conjugations are different than expected.
Lastly, this doesn't really fit anywhere else, but 言いう is often pronounced "ゆう" when spoken.

The non-past tense

{{ imgL }} {{ frog_k }}

Okay, so the dictionary form of any verb is often referred to as the non-past tense.
The non-past form represents an action that has not been completed. However, for most verbs, you don't use the non-past tense to represent a current, ongoing action (notable exceptions being ある and いる), making it almost effectively a future tense in most uses. We'll cover how to represent a presently ongoing action in just a bit.

{{ triL }}

Ren'youkei: The -masu stem

{{ imgR }} {{ frog_s }}

The 連用形れんようけい (conjunctive) form of a verb is also called the -masu stem of the verb, since it's the form of the verb that you attach ます to when making polite verbs. However, there are a few different things that "stem" can refer to in Japanese, so I'll just call it the conjunctive form for clarity's sake.
Before I get into how it's used, let's take a look at how it's formed.
For godan verbs, change the last kana of the dictionary form from the う-row kana to the い-row kana.
For ichidan verbs, simply remove the last る of the dictionary form kana.
する and 来くる, as always, have irregular inflections, changing to し and 来き.

The primary usage of conjunctive form is that you can attach other verbs and verb endings to it, hence the name.
Grammatically speaking, ます (what all polite form verbs end in) is just an auxiliary verb that joins to the conjunctive form to create the meaning of politeness. There are many such verbs that can attach to the conjunctive form, like 込こむ, 出だす, 切きる, that change the meaning of the root.
There are a lot of words that are formed by attaching a noun to the conjunctive form of a verb, too. Some really common ones are 食たべ物もの, 入いり口ぐち, and 出で口ぐち. 方かた is a suffix that can go on the end of a conjunctive-form verb that gives it the meaning "way/method of (verb)ing".
For some verbs, the conjunctive form can also double as a noun, such as in 代かわり, 負まけ, or 踊おどり.
And finally, the conjunctive form of the verb is also used in literary style to link two clauses of a sentence together. In speech or less formal writing, the て form is used for this instead.

私わたしと友ともだちは一緒いっしょに食たべた。 先生せんせいは私わたしとクラスメイトを叱しかった。
My friend and I ate together. The teacher scolded me and a classmate.
卵たまごと牛乳ぎゅうにゅうとアイスを買かう。 カモとカエル、フクロウが一番いちばん好すき。
I'm gonna buy eggs, milk, and ice cream. I like ducks, frogs, and owls the most.
{{ cornerR }}

Once again, と is a particle that has a few other uses, not just this one.
However, one of them is not connecting clauses of a sentence like the English word "and" can. We'll learn ways to connect multiple clauses later, but for now remember that you shouldn't use と to try to try to link clauses. It can only connect nouns together in a list.

{{ triL }}

Non-exhaustive listing using や and とか and など

{{ imgL }} {{ frog_n }}

In English, if we're giving an incomplete list, we might start it off by saying "including", or say "...and others" or "...et cetera" after we finish the list.
In Japanese, we can convey this concept using the や particle.
It works exactly the same as the と particle we just discussed, including the thing about the commas.
とか is a more casual version of や that works basically the same. However, you can also use とか after the last element of your list if you want (it doesn't change the meaning or anything). And finally, if you want to really emphasize the other objects on the list you're leaving out, you can use など after the last word, which basically means et cetera.
If you use とか or など after the last element of your list, you don't (usually) have to use a particle afterwards. Of course, still using the particle is just fine.

{{ triL }} {{ reibuntop }}
木村きむらや雪子ゆきこは英語英語が話はなせる。 音楽おんがくはロックとかメタルとか（が）好すき。
Kimura and Yukiko (and others) can speak English. I like stuff like metal and rock music.
あの店みせはパンやケーキやクッキーなどを売うる。 蛍光けいこうペンとか付箋紙ふせんしなどは意外いがいと便利べんり 。

Other uses of とか and など

{{ imgR }} {{ frog_sh }}

While we're here, I want to note some other uses of とか and など that I think are still pretty related to their normal meanings and are also useful.
You can use both particles after just one word, which still has the same meaning of "stuff like" or "et cetera".
Both can also be used together with the の particle we discussed earlier, which lets you be even more flexible when modifying nouns.
など is also often used to emphasize that one doesn't or shouldn't do something, or anything close to it.
I don't think とか is really used for that, but I'm not sure. In the below sentences you can use either など or とか except for the last one (probably).

{{ triR }} {{ reibuntop }}
ぷよぷよとかのパズルゲームが分わからない。 数十年すうじゅうねん前まえ、携帯けいたいなど存在そんざいしなかった。
I don't understand puzzle games like Puyo Puyo. Decades ago, there weren't things like cell phones.
迷信めいしんとか信しんじない。 あいつは人ひとの気持きもちなど気きにかけない。
I don't believe in superstitions or anything like that.
*using など will sound a little more dismissive of superstitions, I think		 They don't even care about people's feelings.
