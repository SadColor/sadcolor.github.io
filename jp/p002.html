the copula: である

繋辞けいじ：である

Hiya! Welcome back for another lesson!
This is gonna be a quick one about about the Japanese copula, である.

What the heck is a copula?

A copula is a word that connects a noun in the subject to a predicate noun or predicate adjective.
In school, you've probably heard it called a linking verb.

EXAMPLE
My cat is fluffy. Bananas are fruit.

In Japanese, the copula can take many forms, but let's look at the root form: である.
である is formed from the particle で, which connects with nouns and な adjectives, and the verb ある, which means "to be".
When you're writing in a literary style, you will put this after a sentence that ends with a noun or な adjective.
You cannot use である after an い adjective or verb!

EXAMPLE
吾輩わがはいは猫ねこである。 天国てんごくは平和へいわである。
I am a cat.
(this is the name of a book btw)		 Heaven is peaceful.

Root form? What are the other forms?

As stated before である is primarily used in literary writing. Most of the time, it's contracted to だ.
It's used the same way, but also note that in casual speech, sentence-ending だ is usually omitted.
In the future, we'll cover other situations where だ is required.

EXAMPLE
これは終おわりだ。 プリンが好すきだ。
This is the end. I like pudding.

In any situation where you can omit だ, usually including it sounds more assertive and masculine and omitting it sounds more feminine. Ain't gender norms fun?
These situations include: normal casual speech (usually only used for emphasis), with the particles よ and ね (which come after a sentence-ending だ), and when using the explanatory particle の (comes before だ). We haven't covered any of those particles yet, but keep it in mind for later. Or just come back and review this lesson, or something.

While we haven't covered verb conjugation,
I still wanna talk about the past and negative forms of である.
The verb ある conjugates to the past tense あった and the negative form ない (it's one of few irregular verbs).
Consequently, the past and negative forms of である are であった and ではない, which are contracted to だった and じゃない.
Unlike だ, there is no situation where you can omit だった or じゃない, because they serve the function of indicating tense and negating.

そのテストは楽らくだった。 イルカは魚さかなじゃない。
That test was easy. Dolphins aren't fish.

Why does a は particle sneak into ではない when it seems like it should be just でない?
To be honest, I'm not really sure. Sorry...!
It's uncommon, but in writing you can see でない without a は when used in a subordinate clause.

You're probably familiar with です, a word that serves the same function.
です is the polite form of である.
ある conjugates to あります in the polite form, ありました in the polite past tense, and ありません in the past tense. Therefore, である becomes であります, でありました, and ではありません, which contract to です, でした, and じゃありません.
However, an important difference with である/だ is that you can use です after い adjectives! Similar to the earlier point about だった, this is because it serves the function of indicating politeness. In fact, it's the only way to make a polite sentence from an い adjective predicate.

その服は可愛かわいいです。 変へんな感かんじでした。 料理りょうりは簡単かんたんじゃありません。
Those clothes are cute. It was a weird feeling. Cooking isn't easy.

い adjectives have their own past and negative conjugations, so you cannot use でした and じゃありません with い adjectives to indicate past tense or negation. We'll cover this in the next lesson about adjectives.

I think that's about it. It may not seem that important, but I hope knowing that です and its many forms come from something can help you make some connections or alleviate confusion further down the road.
